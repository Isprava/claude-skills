# Construction Safety & Compliance Skill

A framework for maintaining a safe construction site that meets all legal WHS/OHS obligations, protects workers, and avoids the devastating human and commercial cost of preventable incidents. Safety is not a compliance checkbox — it is the foundation of a professional construction operation.

## Core Principle

**Safety is a system, not a slogan.** Incidents happen when systems fail — not just when individuals make mistakes. Effective safety management identifies hazards before they cause harm, builds controls into the work method, and creates a culture where every worker feels responsible for safety and empowered to raise concerns.

## Scoring

**Goal: 10/10.** When auditing or planning site safety, rate 0–10 based on hazard identification, adequacy of controls, documentation completeness, worker engagement, and legal compliance. A 10/10 means all high-risk activities have written SWMSs, all workers are inducted, the site is audit-ready at any time, and near-misses are reported and acted on.

## Safety Framework

### 1. Site Safety Management Plan (SSMP)

**Core concept:** The overarching document that describes how safety will be managed on this specific project.

**Why it matters:** Required by law for construction projects above certain thresholds. It is the evidence that safety has been systematically considered.

**SSMP must-haves:**
- Project description and site details
- Roles and responsibilities (Principal Contractor, HSR, first aid officers)
- Site-specific hazard register
- Emergency procedures (fire, medical, structural collapse, utility strike)
- Induction requirements
- Incident reporting procedure
- Subcontractor safety management obligations
- Safety inspection schedule
- PPE requirements by work area

### 2. Hazard Identification & Risk Assessment

**Core concept:** A systematic process to identify all hazards on site, assess their risk, and implement controls.

**Why it matters:** You cannot control a hazard you haven't identified.

**Hierarchy of controls (most to least effective):**

| Level | Control Type | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| 1 | Eliminate | Remove the hazard entirely |
| 2 | Substitute | Replace with a less hazardous material or method |
| 3 | Isolate | Barrier, exclusion zone, enclosure |
| 4 | Engineering | Guard, interlock, ventilation system |
| 5 | Administrative | Safe work procedure, training, permit to work |
| 6 | PPE | Hard hat, harness, gloves (last resort only) |

**Site hazard register categories:**
- Working at height (falls)
- Excavations and trenches (collapse)
- Electrical (overhead lines, live conductors)
- Plant and equipment (cranes, EWPs, excavators)
- Hazardous materials (asbestos, silica dust, lead)
- Manual handling
- Hot work (welding, cutting)
- Confined spaces
- Utilities (underground services)

### 3. Safe Work Method Statements (SWMS)

**Core concept:** A written document required for all high-risk construction work (HRCW) that identifies hazards and describes the safe method of work.

**Why it matters:** SWMS are legally required for HRCW. They also protect workers by ensuring everyone knows the safe procedure before starting.

**High-risk construction work (HRCW) — SWMS always required:**
- Work at height > 2m
- Excavations > 1.5m deep
- Work in or near confined spaces
- Work near energised electrical installations
- Work near pressurised gas distribution mains
- Demolition work
- Work on or near chemical, fuel, or refrigerant lines
- Work involving explosives
- Work on telecommunications towers
- Tilt-up or precast concrete elements
- Work in areas with artificial extremes of temperature
- Work near traffic or mobile plant

**SWMS structure:**
1. Activity description
2. Plant, tools, and equipment required
3. Training and competency requirements
4. PPE requirements
5. Step-by-step work sequence
6. Hazard identification per step
7. Risk rating (L x C = R) before and after controls
8. Controls to be applied
9. Emergency procedure
10. Signoff by workers performing the task

### 4. Inductions

**Core concept:** Every person on site must be inducted before they start work.

**Why it matters:** Uninducted workers are statistically the most at-risk. Induction ensures everyone knows the site rules, hazards, and emergency procedures.

**Induction types:**

| Type | Who | When | Duration |
|------|-----|------|----------|
| Site induction | All workers and visitors | Before first entry to site | 30–60 min |
| Subcontractor induction | All sub workers | Before starting work | Includes site + task-specific |
| Visitor induction | Clients, inspectors, visitors | Before site entry | 10 min minimum |
| Task-specific induction | Workers on HRCW | Before starting the specific task | Per SWMS |

**Induction records:** Keep signed records for every inductee for the duration of the project + 5 years.

### 5. Toolbox Talks

**Core concept:** Brief (5–15 min), regular safety conversations with the site team on a specific safety topic.

**Why it matters:** Toolbox talks keep safety top of mind, address site-specific hazards in real time, and demonstrate an active safety culture.

**Best practices:**
- Weekly minimum; daily during high-risk phases
- Topic should be relevant to current site activities
- Interactive — ask questions, don't just lecture
- Record attendance and topic
- Address recent near-misses or incidents on site or in the industry

### 6. Incident Reporting & Investigation

**Core concept:** All incidents, near-misses, and hazard observations must be reported, investigated, and acted on.

**Why it matters:** Near-misses are free lessons. Ignored near-misses become incidents. Ignored incidents become fatalities.

**Incident response sequence:**
1. Preserve life — first aid, call emergency services
2. Secure the scene — prevent further harm
3. Notify the Principal Contractor, HSR, and insurer
4. Report to the regulator (notifiable incidents within 24 hours — death, serious injury, dangerous incident)
5. Investigate root cause (use the 5-Whys or fault tree analysis)
6. Implement corrective actions
7. Communicate learnings to site team

**Notifiable incidents (must be reported to WHS regulator):**
- Death of any person
- Serious injury or illness (hospitalization, amputation, serious head/eye/spinal injury)
- Dangerous incident (near-miss that could have caused death or serious injury)

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| SWMS filed but not followed | Document compliance without actual safety | Review SWMS at pre-start; workers must confirm they've read and understood it |
| PPE as the only control | Least effective control; relies on human compliance | Apply hierarchy: eliminate/substitute/isolate before relying on PPE |
| Near-misses not reported | Misses the learning opportunity; culture of silence | Make reporting easy and non-punitive; celebrate near-miss reports |
| Induction records not kept | Legal liability; cannot prove compliance | Use a digital sign-in system that automatically records inductions |
| Toolbox talks are one-way lectures | Low retention, low engagement | Ask questions; use real site examples; keep it to 10 minutes |

## Quick Diagnostic

| Question | If No | Action |
|----------|-------|--------|
| Does every HRCW activity have a current, signed SWMS? | Legal non-compliance | Stop the activity; complete and sign the SWMS |
| Are all workers signed into today's induction register? | Uninducted person on site | Remove from site until inducted |
| Has there been a toolbox talk this week? | Safety culture lapsing | Schedule and deliver one today |
| Are near-misses being reported? | Under-reporting culture | Review barriers to reporting; reinforce with team |
| Is the emergency procedure posted and communicated? | Workers don't know what to do | Brief team and post procedure at site entry |
