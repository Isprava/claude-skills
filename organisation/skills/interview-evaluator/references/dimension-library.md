# Dimension Library

Pre-written dimension definitions for common role types. Use as a starting point — always adapt to the specific role and JD before using. Never copy verbatim without reviewing fit.

---

## Execution orientation

**When to use:** Any role where the candidate receives direction and must deliver output independently. Execution, ops, coordinator, associate PM, program manager, EA.

**Why it matters (template):** "[Role] is primarily a delivery function. The hiring manager defines what; this person figures out how and ships it. Failure in this dimension looks like: tasks that stall without check-ins, deliverables that need to be redone for structure, blockers that get escalated instead of routed."

**Core questions:**
- "Walk me through [specific project from resume] — not what it was, but how you actually built it. Where did you get stuck and what did you do?"
- "Give me a situation where you were given a task with unclear instructions and had to figure it out yourself. Walk me through each step."
- "I'm going to give you a task right now — [role-specific task]. Walk me through exactly how you'd approach it from the moment I finish speaking."

**Green flags (observable):**
- Narrates blockers and their resolution at granular level
- Defines output format before being asked
- Asks one clarifying question before starting the task
- Prioritises steps — knows what to do first vs last

**Red flags (observable):**
- Project description stays at outcome level — no process detail
- Output = "a summary" with no structure described
- First response to ambiguity is to ask for more instructions
- Cannot describe the last thing they delivered in concrete terms

## AI / tech conceptual fluency

**When to use:** Any role adjacent to AI, data, engineering, or technical product. The candidate doesn't need to code — but needs a systems mental model.

**Why it matters (template):** "This person will coordinate with engineers and vendors on AI solutions. Without a working mental model of how systems communicate (APIs), where data lives (cloud, databases), and what agents actually do, they will be a relay rather than a collaborator. Failure looks like: missed requirements, misunderstood technical constraints, over-reliance on engineers to interpret everything."

**Core questions:**
- "How do you think a tool like ChatGPT actually works? Walk me through it — I don't need exact technical detail, I want to hear how you reason about it."
- "If we needed to connect our internal tool to an external AI service — what do you think the pieces involved would be, even roughly?"
- "You've used [tool from resume]. What's the difference between running that locally and deploying it as a service that runs in the cloud?"

**Green flags (observable):**
- Can distinguish LLM from search engine conceptually
- Uses words like "request", "response", "credentials", "environment" even loosely
- Understands the difference between a script and a deployed service
- Asks a clarifying follow-up that shows systems awareness

**Red flags (observable):**
- "ChatGPT searches the internet and gives answers"
- Cannot describe what an API does even loosely
- Has never thought about where their local data files "live"
- Freezes entirely on any question outside exact tools used

## Learning agility

**When to use:** Any role where the candidate's current knowledge is below what the role requires, but the pace of the environment demands fast self-directed ramp. Early-career hires, career switchers, fast-growing teams.

**Why it matters (template):** "This hire is a learning bet. The role requires [X] which [candidate name] doesn't currently have. The question isn't whether they know it — it's whether they can acquire it fast enough, without a classroom, without being told what to learn. Failure looks like: still waiting to be trained on month 3."

**Core questions:**
- "Tell me about the hardest technical concept you've ever taught yourself — not in a course, but because you needed it. What was your method and how long did it take?"
- "If you joined tomorrow and needed to get up to speed on [specific domain] well enough to be useful in 30 days — what is your plan? Be specific."

**Green flags (observable):**
- Names a specific concept they self-learned with a specific method
- Learning plan names actual resources (docs, repos, specific tools) not just "YouTube"
- Includes a milestone — "by day 7 I'd have done X"
- Plans to build something, not just consume content

**Red flags (observable):**
- All learning has been structured and course-delivered
- Learning plan = "I'd watch tutorials" with no named source
- Cannot name a single specific resource for the domain
- Output is consumption-only — no applying or building

## Research quality & synthesis

**When to use:** Any role that requires investigating options, comparing vendors, evaluating approaches, or generating a recommendation from unstructured information.

**Why it matters (template):** "A core deliverable in this role is structured research — comparing options, recommending one, and being able to defend it. The failure mode is: researching until there's too much information, producing no recommendation, or producing a recommendation that can't survive a single push-back."

**Core questions:**
- "Show me or describe the last piece of research you did where you had to compare multiple options and recommend one. What was your process and what did you produce?"
- "How do you decide when you know enough to form a confident opinion vs when you need to keep digging?"

**Green flags (observable):**
- Defines criteria before comparing options
- Produces structured output — a table, a doc, a slide — not notes
- Cross-references multiple sources before concluding
- Willing to defend a recommendation when pushed

**Red flags (observable):**
- Research = first page of results
- Output is always informal — a verbal update or a raw notes dump
- Avoids taking a position: "I'd present both sides and let them decide"
- Cannot describe how they assess source credibility

## Stakeholder communication

**When to use:** Roles that require getting work done through people who don't report to them, or presenting findings / recommendations to senior stakeholders.

**Why it matters (template):** "This person will need to get responses, decisions, and input from people who are senior to them and not accountable to them. Communication failure looks like: long emails that don't get replied to, presentations that don't drive a decision, people who don't know what they're being asked."

**Core questions:**
- "Tell me about a time you needed something from a senior person who wasn't prioritising you. What did you do?"
- "Describe a piece of communication you wrote — email, doc, slide — that you're proud of. Why was it effective?"

**Green flags (observable):**
- Leads with the ask before the context in their communication instinct
- Distinguishes what a senior stakeholder needs vs what a peer needs
- Has an explicit strategy for getting responses — not just "following up"
- Can describe the structure of a communication before its content

**Red flags (observable):**
- "I'd send a detailed email explaining everything"
- Escalates to their manager as first option when stakeholder doesn't respond
- Communication strategy = volume (more emails, more messages)
- Cannot describe what the recipient needs vs what they want to say

## Domain-specific curiosity (AI)

**When to use:** Roles adjacent to AI product, AI strategy, or AI operations where the candidate has limited AI exposure but the role demands genuine curiosity to self-develop.

**Why it matters (template):** "We're not hiring for AI expertise — we're hiring for AI curiosity. The role will put this person in rooms where AI initiatives are being defined, debated, and executed. A candidate with no genuine curiosity will fall behind within 60 days. A candidate who self-educates will become a force multiplier."

**Core questions:**
- "What's the most interesting thing you've read or tried related to AI in the last 3 months? Walk me through it."
- "What do you think an AI agent is? Describe it like I know nothing about tech."
- "If you had to explain to your parents what large language models do, what would you say?"

**Green flags (observable):**
- Has used AI tools beyond casual chatting — applied them to their own work
- Has a mental model of LLMs that is roughly correct (prediction, not search)
- Can describe an agent in terms of autonomy and multi-step action
- Has an opinion on what AI can and can't do well

**Red flags (observable):**
- "ChatGPT is basically a very smart search engine"
- Has used AI only to summarise text or generate generic content
- No self-directed exploration — only uses what their course/job required
- Cannot form an opinion on agents even when given a hypothetical

## Prioritisation & scope management

**When to use:** PM roles, program managers, chiefs of staff, anyone managing multiple workstreams.

**Why it matters (template):** "This role carries multiple active workstreams simultaneously. Failure looks like: everything feels urgent, nothing gets finished, or the highest-visibility task always wins regardless of importance."

**Core questions:**
- "Walk me through how you managed your time across your [most recent or most complex project from resume]. How did you decide what to work on each day?"
- "Tell me about a time you had more to do than you could finish. What did you cut and how did you make that call?"

**Green flags (observable):**
- Has an explicit prioritisation method — not just "I make lists"
- Can articulate a trade-off they made and why
- Says no (or "not yet") to things and explains the criteria
- Tracks their own progress without being asked

**Red flags (observable):**
- "I just get through whatever comes first"
- Cannot name a specific thing they've deprioritised
- Prioritisation logic is seniority of the requester, not importance of the task
- No system — just reactive to whatever is most recent or loudest
