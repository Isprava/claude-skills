# Proposal Writing Skill

A framework for writing proposals that win business by focusing on the client's problem — not on the company's capabilities. Great proposals make the client feel understood, demonstrate clear value, and make the decision to proceed feel obvious and low-risk.

## Core Principle

**The proposal is not about you — it's about them.** Proposals that lead with "We are a leading firm with 20 years of experience…" lose. Proposals that lead with "You are facing X problem, which is costing you Y, and here is exactly how we will solve it" win.

## Scoring

**Goal: 10/10.** When reviewing or writing a proposal, rate 0–10 based on client focus, clarity of problem statement, specificity of solution, credibility of proof, and strength of CTA. A 10/10 means the client reads the proposal and thinks "they get us completely."

## Proposal Structure

### 1. Executive Summary (The Decision-Maker Page)

**Core concept:** A one-page summary that captures the entire proposal for the executive who won't read the rest.

**Why it matters:** Decision-makers often only read the executive summary. It must stand alone.

**Executive summary structure:**
1. **Their situation** (1 sentence): What is happening in their business context?
2. **Their problem** (2–3 sentences): What specific challenge are they facing?
3. **Our solution** (2–3 sentences): What we will do to solve it
4. **Expected outcome** (1–2 sentences): What result they will achieve
5. **Investment** (1 sentence): Total cost and timeline
6. **Next step** (1 sentence): What we need from them to proceed

### 2. Understanding of the Problem

**Core concept:** Demonstrate that you understand the client's situation better than they expected.

**Why it matters:** Trust is built when the client feels understood. A detailed, accurate problem statement signals expertise.

**Key practices:**
- Use the client's own language from discovery conversations
- Include context beyond just what they told you — show you understand their industry
- Quantify the cost or impact of the problem where possible
- Avoid generic problem statements that could apply to any company

**Template:**
> "Based on our conversations, [Company Name] is facing [specific challenge]. This is resulting in [measurable impact]. The root cause appears to be [underlying issue]. Left unaddressed, this will [future consequence]."

### 3. Proposed Solution

**Core concept:** A specific, detailed description of exactly what you will deliver — mapped to their stated problem.

**Why it matters:** Vague solutions create doubt; specific solutions build confidence.

**Solution section must-haves:**
- What you will do (scope of work)
- How you will do it (methodology, process)
- What they need to provide or do (client responsibilities)
- What they will NOT get (clear scope boundaries)
- Timeline with milestones

**Avoid:**
- Feature lists that aren't mapped to their problems
- Jargon the client doesn't use
- Vague language ("we will work with you to improve…") — be specific

### 4. Deliverables & Timeline

**Core concept:** A clear list of what the client receives and when.

**Why it matters:** Clients want to know exactly what they are buying.

**Format:**

| Milestone | Deliverable | Timeline |
|-----------|------------|----------|
| Kickoff | Project plan, team introductions | Week 1 |
| Phase 1 complete | [Specific deliverable] | Week 4 |
| Phase 2 complete | [Specific deliverable] | Week 8 |
| Final delivery | [Final deliverable + handover] | Week 12 |

### 5. Proof & Credibility

**Core concept:** Evidence that you can do what you say — without bragging.

**Why it matters:** Claims without proof are noise. Proof without bragging is persuasion.

**Proof formats (ranked by impact):**

| Type | Impact | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| Case study (same industry) | Highest | "We delivered X for [similar company], resulting in Y" |
| Testimonial | High | Direct quote from a satisfied client |
| Data / metrics | High | "Our average client sees 30% cost reduction in 90 days" |
| Client logos | Medium | "Trusted by Company A, B, C" |
| Team credentials | Lower | Bios, certifications, years of experience |

### 6. Investment (Pricing)

**Core concept:** Pricing presented clearly, confidently, and framed around value — not cost.

**Why it matters:** How you present pricing shapes how it is perceived.

**Pricing best practices:**
- Always frame pricing after establishing value (ROI, cost of the problem)
- Present options (Good / Better / Best) to anchor to the middle
- Break down what is included — show the work behind the number
- Be specific about payment terms and what triggers each payment
- Don't apologize for your price

**Value framing example:**
> "Addressing [problem] is currently costing your operation approximately [X] per year. Our engagement investment of [Y] is designed to recover that cost within [Z months] and deliver ongoing savings beyond the engagement."

### 7. Risk Reduction

**Core concept:** Proactively address the client's fear of making the wrong decision.

**Why it matters:** Buyers are often not afraid of the cost — they're afraid of failure. Remove that fear.

**Risk reducers:**
- Phased engagement (start small, expand)
- Milestone-based payment (pay as deliverables are achieved)
- Clear exit clauses
- Warranty / guarantee on deliverables
- References they can call
- Pilot or proof-of-concept option

### 8. Clear Next Step (CTA)

**Core concept:** Tell the client exactly what to do next to proceed — make it frictionless.

**Why it matters:** Proposals that end vaguely ("let us know if you have questions") stall.

**Strong CTAs:**
- "Sign and return the attached agreement to kick off your project by [date]"
- "Schedule a 30-minute call with [name] to finalize scope: [calendar link]"
- "Reply to this email with your approval to begin Phase 1"

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Opening with company history / credentials | Client doesn't care about you yet | Open with their problem |
| No executive summary | Decision-maker won't read the full doc | Always include a one-page summary |
| Generic solution section | Looks like a copy-paste template | Every section should reference their specific situation |
| Pricing with no context | Client has no anchor for value | Always present ROI/value before the price |
| No clear next step | Proposal gathers dust | End with one specific action the client should take |
| Too long | Decision-maker won't read it | Aim for 5–8 pages max; appendix for detail |

## Quick Diagnostic

| Question | If No | Action |
|----------|-------|--------|
| Does the proposal open with the client's problem? | It's about you, not them | Rewrite the opening around their challenge |
| Is the solution section specific to this client? | Generic proposal | Add references to their specific situation, language, and goals |
| Is there a clear ROI or value statement before pricing? | Price shock risk | Add a value framing paragraph before the pricing table |
| Is there one clear next step? | Proposal will stall | Add a specific CTA with a deadline |
