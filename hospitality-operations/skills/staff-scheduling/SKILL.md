# Staff Scheduling Skill

A framework for building hospitality rosters that deliver the right service levels at the right labour cost while keeping staff engaged and compliant with employment law. Great scheduling is a balance between operational needs, financial targets, and employee experience — and it is one of the most leveraged management skills in hospitality.

## Core Principle

**Schedule to demand, not to habit.** The biggest scheduling mistake is running last week's roster this week. Hospitality demand is driven by occupancy, bookings, events, and seasons — your labour must flex with it. Intelligent scheduling turns labour from a fixed cost into a competitive advantage.

## Scoring

**Goal: 10/10.** When reviewing or building a roster, rate 0–10 based on demand alignment, labour cost efficiency, award compliance, staff preference accommodation, and coverage at all critical service times. A 10/10 means service levels are met, labour cost is within budget, no compliance risks, and staff preferences are accommodated where operationally possible.

## Scheduling Framework

### 1. Demand Forecasting

**Core concept:** Predict the staffing demand for each shift based on business volume before building the roster.

**Why it matters:** Scheduling without forecasting is guesswork. Even simple demand forecasting reduces both over- and under-staffing by 20–40%.

**Demand inputs to use:**

| Input | Where to Find | Impact |
|-------|--------------|--------|
| Room occupancy (forecast) | PMS / reservations | Drives housekeeping and F&B breakfast volume |
| Restaurant bookings | Reservation system | Drives F&B floor, kitchen, and bar staffing |
| Events and functions | Events calendar | Peaks in F&B, reception, and porter requirements |
| Historical data (same day last year) | PMS reports | Baseline demand by day of week |
| Local events (concerts, conferences) | Local council / venue calendar | Unexpected demand spikes |

**Labour ratio guidelines (starting points — adjust to your property):**

| Area | Ratio |
|------|-------|
| Housekeeping | 1 room attendant per 12–16 rooms (check-out day) / 16–20 (stay-over) |
| Front desk | 1 agent per 30–40 arrivals (or departures) per hour |
| Restaurant | 1 server per 4–6 tables (fine dining) / 6–8 tables (casual) |
| Kitchen | Based on covers per shift; calibrate from your kitchen manager |

### 2. Roster Construction

**Core concept:** Build the roster by matching shift patterns to demand curves, starting with fixed requirements and filling in around them.

**Why it matters:** Poorly constructed rosters create peaks of under-coverage and troughs of over-staffing in the same day.

**Roster build sequence:**
1. Map the demand curve (busy/slow periods) for each day
2. Identify fixed shifts (management, key roles that must be covered regardless)
3. Place peak-demand shifts at the busiest times
4. Fill gaps with part-time and casual staff
5. Check coverage at every hour of operation — no uncovered critical periods
6. Check for compliance before finalizing (see section 4)
7. Publish with minimum notice period (check your award/EBA)

**Shift pattern types:**

| Pattern | Best Used For |
|---------|--------------|
| Split shift | Breakfast + dinner peaks with gap in between (F&B) |
| Staggered start | Spreading arrivals across a peak period (check-in peaks) |
| Rotating roster | Fair distribution of weekends and public holidays |
| On-call | Low-cost coverage for unpredictable demand |
| Overtime | Last resort for emergency coverage only |

### 3. Labour Cost Management

**Core concept:** Monitor and manage labour cost as a percentage of revenue in real time — not just at month end.

**Why it matters:** Labour is typically 30–40% of hospitality revenue. Even small inefficiencies compound into significant profit impact.

**Key labour cost metrics:**

| Metric | Formula | Target (typical) |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| Labour cost % | Labour cost / Revenue × 100 | 28–35% (varies by segment) |
| Revenue per labour hour | Revenue / Total hours worked | Benchmark against budget |
| Rooms cleaned per room attendant | Total rooms / Room attendants on shift | 12–16 check-outs |
| Covers per server | Total covers / Floor staff on shift | 20–30 covers per server |

**Labour cost levers:**

| Lever | Impact | Caution |
|-------|--------|---------|
| Reduce overtime | High impact, quick win | Don't create under-coverage |
| Shift permanent to casual | Reduces fixed cost | Increases scheduling complexity |
| Cross-train staff | Covers multiple areas with fewer staff | Training investment required |
| Align rosters with demand forecast | Structural fix | Requires discipline; resist habit-based scheduling |

### 4. Award & EBA Compliance

**Core concept:** Every roster must comply with the applicable Modern Award or Enterprise Bargaining Agreement before it is published.

**Why it matters:** Non-compliant rosters create legal liability, back-pay claims, and reputational damage.

**Key compliance checks (Hospitality Industry General Award — verify against your applicable award):**

| Requirement | Typical Rule | Check |
|------------|-------------|-------|
| Minimum shift length | 3 hours minimum | No shift shorter than 3 hours |
| Break between shifts | 10–12 hours minimum | No less than 10h between finish and next start |
| Maximum daily hours | 10–12 hours (ordinary + reasonable overtime) | Flag shifts over 10 hours |
| Roster notice | 7 days minimum (confirm in your award) | Publish roster at least 7 days in advance |
| Rest period | 2 consecutive days off per week (or 2 days in 14) | Confirm per roster cycle |
| Penalty rates | Evenings, weekends, public holidays attract loadings | Accounted for in labour cost calculations |

**Important:** Always verify requirements against the specific Modern Award or EBA applicable to your property and jurisdiction.

See: [references/award-compliance-checklist.md](references/award-compliance-checklist.md)

### 5. Staff Communication & Wellbeing

**Core concept:** Rosters should be published early, communicated clearly, and built with reasonable accommodation of staff preferences.

**Why it matters:** Poor roster communication and no regard for staff preferences drive absenteeism and turnover — both of which increase labour costs more than any scheduling saving.

**Best practices:**
- Publish rosters at least 7 days in advance (more where possible)
- Collect availability and preference requests at least 2 weeks before the roster period
- Rotate unpopular shifts (early mornings, late nights, weekends) fairly
- Provide a clear process for shift swaps that maintains compliance
- Respond to availability change requests within 24 hours
- Recognize staff who cover last-minute shifts

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Copying last week's roster | Ignores demand changes; creates consistent over/under-staffing | Forecast demand; rebuild each week from the demand curve |
| Scheduling to minimum coverage | No buffer for absenteeism; one call-in creates a crisis | Build a 10–15% buffer via casuals or on-call staff |
| Publishing roster with <48h notice | Staff can't plan personal lives; morale damage | Commit to 7-day notice as a minimum standard |
| Ignoring overtime until month end | Overtime blowouts are invisible until it's too late | Track overtime in real time; cap with manager approval |
| No compliance check before publishing | Legal liability and financial risk | Compliance checklist required before any roster is published |

## Quick Diagnostic

| Question | If No | Action |
|----------|-------|--------|
| Is the roster built from a demand forecast? | Scheduling by habit | Pull occupancy and covers forecast; rebuild from demand |
| Is the roster published at least 7 days in advance? | Non-compliant; staff morale issue | Commit to 7-day notice; build the roster process to enable it |
| Is labour cost % tracked against budget weekly? | Surprises at month end | Add weekly labour cost review to the management routine |
| Has the roster been compliance-checked against the award? | Legal risk | Run the compliance checklist before publishing |
